Michigan Mobile Home Skirting for Two Very Different Climates

Michigan has one of the largest concentrations of manufactured homes in the Midwest, yet the state presents two dramatically different climate challenges. The Upper Peninsula rivals Minnesota for cold and snow, with Sault Ste. Marie averaging 143 inches of snow annually and winter temperatures regularly dropping below negative 20 degrees Fahrenheit. The Lower Peninsula, milder by comparison, still endures lake-effect snow and wind off Lake Michigan and Lake Huron that make winter skirting selection critical.

From the Upper Peninsula to the Lower Peninsula, Michigan winters demand more than standard vinyl skirting can deliver. Lake-effect snow deposits heavy, wet snow quickly. Cold temperatures penetrate skirting joints and vents. Wind-driven moisture finds gaps and causes freeze-thaw cycles that split inferior panels. The right skirting product, properly installed, protects against both cold and the moisture intrusion that drives pipe freeze-ups.

Michigan's Climate: Two Very Different Regions

Upper Peninsula: Extreme Winter Conditions

The Upper Peninsula is one of the snowiest regions east of the Rocky Mountains. Sault Ste. Marie averages 143 inches of snow per year. Marquette, in the central UP, averages 200 inches annually due to lake-effect snow off Lake Superior. Winter temperatures regularly drop to negative 10, negative 20, and even negative 30 degrees Fahrenheit.

This is not a region where non-insulated vinyl survives. Freeze-thaw cycles expand and contract panels repeatedly. Plastic becomes brittle. Seals fail. Water intrusion behind skirting can lead to pipe ruptures that cost thousands to repair.

Insulated skirting is the baseline requirement in the UP. Not a luxury, not an option. The insulation protects against the extreme cold and, equally important, reduces the interior-to-exterior temperature gradient that drives moisture infiltration and frost formation inside the skirt cavity.

Lower Peninsula: Lake-Effect Snow and Wind

The Lower Peninsula is divided into distinct zones. Northern areas (Traverse City, Petoskey, Gaylord) receive heavy lake-effect snow from Lake Michigan. Grand Rapids, Muskegon, and Kalamazoo in the west face sustained moisture and cold. The southern LP (Detroit, Flint, Lansing, Ann Arbor) is milder but still experiences winter lows in the single digits or below zero.

Lake-effect snow is a specific challenge. When cold Arctic air moves over warm Great Lakes water, it picks up moisture and deposits it downwind as intense, heavy snow. This wet snow clings to skirting, accumulates along top rails, and creates weight loads that standard vinyl cannot withstand. Seams and vents are particularly vulnerable to moisture intrusion from wind-driven snow.

Even in southern Michigan, winter temperatures regularly dip below freezing. Any skirting that allows water behind panels risks freeze-thaw damage and pipe ruptures. Insulated skirting is a strong choice across the entire Lower Peninsula, and essential in the northern third.

Lake-Effect Snow and Skirting Installation

Lake-effect snow events are unique to regions adjacent to the Great Lakes. Wind-driven moisture rapidly deposits heavy, wet snow. Unlike typical snow, lake-effect events often occur without the wind that dries and compacts snow elsewhere. The result is wet, dense snow that clings to skirting top rails and accumulates in seams and around vent openings.

This moisture, combined with freeze-thaw cycles, is the primary threat to skirting in Michigan. Snow sitting against skirting joints for weeks creates hydrostatic pressure. Wind-driven snow finds gaps in poorly sealed vents and access doors. Water behind panels freezes, expands, and splits vinyl or opens seams.

The Everlock rail system is designed with tight panel connections and top-rail seals that shed water rather than trap it. However, proper installation matters. Vents must be firmly mounted and seals must be tight. Access doors must have proper gaskets. The vent covers that you install should be rated for snow load and wind, and must be checked annually for gaps or damage.

Blocked vents under snow accumulation create a second problem: condensation inside the skirt cavity. If Arctic air cannot circulate under the home, moisture from the ground and from exterior snow melt collects and refreezes on pipes. This is why vent placement and clearing snow from vents is critical in the UP and northern LP.

Insulated vs Non-Insulated Skirting for Michigan

The insulation in products like Thermal EverRock and Gorilla Insulated serves two primary functions in Michigan. First, it reduces the exterior cold from reaching the area under the home, which protects water pipes from freezing and reduces heating costs. Second, insulation reduces condensation by preventing extreme temperature gradients inside the skirt cavity.

In the Upper Peninsula, where winter temperatures regularly reach negative 20 degrees or lower, non-insulated vinyl cannot protect pipes without supplemental heat tape and managed venting. The risk of pipe rupture is simply too high.

In the Lower Peninsula, the choice depends on your location and your home's pier height. Homes in northern Michigan regions or homes elevated more than 18 inches should have insulated skirting. In southern Michigan, insulated skirting is not mandatory, but it pays for itself over time in reduced heating bills and eliminated freeze-up calls. Most Michigan homeowners who experience one winter with pipe freeze issues will retrofit insulated skirting the following spring.

Thermal EverRock provides 1.5 inches of rigid foam insulation. Gorilla Insulated Skirting offers higher density insulation and heavier panels, making it ideal for the harshest UP locations.

Pipe Freeze Prevention in Michigan

Michigan's combination of extreme cold and lake-effect moisture creates a unique pipe-freeze challenge. A comprehensive approach includes four elements.

First: insulated skirting. This is the foundation. Insulation slows heat loss and prevents the interior-to-exterior temperature gradient that drives condensation.

Second: heat tape. Wrap exposed pipes with thermostat-controlled heat tape. Attach the tape to the water supply line and run it into an outlet inside the home. Heat tape activates when temperatures drop below 50 degrees and maintains flow.

Third: closed vents in winter. During winter, close foundation vents to prevent Arctic air from flowing under the home and around pipes. Leave a small gap (roughly 2 inches) or a low-level vent open to allow moisture to escape. In spring, fully open all vents.

Fourth: moisture management. If you live in the UP or northern LP, check after major lake-effect snow events to ensure vents are not blocked by snow accumulation. Clear vents immediately to restore air circulation.

Many Michigan homeowners also use foam pipe insulation (1.5-inch wall thickness) in addition to heat tape for exposed runs. The combination of insulated skirting, heat tape, managed venting, and foam pipe wrap creates a robust freeze-prevention system that works year after year.

HOA Communities in Michigan

Michigan has many manufactured home communities with appearance standards. Appearance codes may require matching skirting color, continuous perimeter coverage, visible access doors, and prohibition of damaged or worn-looking panels. Some HOAs also specify products, preferring rigid composite materials over lightweight vinyl.

For HOA communities where appearance standards are strict, EverRock (available in stone textures) and the Novik line (faux stone and brick) offer both durability and curb appeal. These products satisfy appearance requirements and deliver the cold-climate performance Michigan requires.

Always review your community's skirting rules before ordering. Contact the community office to confirm which products are approved. A few minutes of research now prevents having to replace skirting after purchase.

HUD Requirements and Michigan Code

All manufactured homes must meet HUD Manufactured Home Construction and Safety Standards for skirting enclosure. The standards require full perimeter coverage, functional access door, and durable materials. Michigan does not impose additional state skirting codes beyond HUD standards.

However, if you are financing through an FHA or VA loan, the lender's appraiser will inspect skirting as part of the home appraisal. An appraisal can fail if skirting is missing, damaged, or deteriorated. Verify your lender's standards before ordering.

Michigan Mobile Home Skirting: Common Questions

Do I need insulated skirting in Michigan? +

In the Upper Peninsula and northern Lower Peninsula, yes. Insulated skirting is mandatory for pipe protection. In southern Michigan, insulated skirting is a strong choice. It pays for itself over time in reduced heating costs and eliminates freeze-up emergencies. If your home is elevated more than 18 inches, insulated skirting is highly recommended statewide.

What is the difference between Thermal EverRock and Gorilla Insulated? +

Both are insulated vinyl. Thermal EverRock provides 1.5 inches of foam insulation and has a stone-textured appearance. Gorilla Insulated Skirting has heavier gauge panels and higher density insulation (approximately 2 inches). For the Upper Peninsula or an exposed northern Lower Peninsula location, Gorilla is worth the small additional cost for maximum cold protection and durability.

Does lake-effect snow damage skirting? +

Lake-effect snow itself does not damage quality skirting. The risk is moisture intrusion at joints and vents if installation is not tight. Proper installation, with sealed vents and firm panel connections, handles lake-effect snow without damage. Regularly clearing vents and access doors of snow accumulation prevents condensation and moisture problems inside the skirt cavity.
